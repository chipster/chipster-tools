Fold change (FC)

Fold change (FC) describes the ratio of two values i.e. (expression in condition 1)/(expression in condition 2). Fold changes are often reported in (2-based) logarithmic scale, because that way they are nicely centered around 0, so that increase in expression shows positive FC and descrease negative. This is easier to understand and nicer to plot!

log2FC is simply computed just log2(condition1/condition2). You can however see in some of our codes and elsewhere also the format log2(condition1) - log2(condition2), which leans on the logarithm rule: log(A/B) = log (A) - log(B). So same thing and result, just computed in two different ways.

To switch back to FC from log2FC, the formula is log2(x)=y <=> 2^y=x. So for example:

  • log2FC = 3 equals to FC=2^3=8, which means that the expression in condition 1 is 8 times higher than in condition 2.
  • log2FC = 1 means that FC = 2, which means that expression in condition 1 is twice as much as in condition 2, or 200%.
  • log2FC = -1 means that FC = 0.5, which means that expression in condition 1 is half as much as in condition 2, or 50%.

p-value

p-value is the probability of obtaining test results at least as extreme as the results actually observed (=the measured expression values). So the larger the p-value, the higher the likelihood that the gene is in the list of differentially expressed genes just bey chance. So the small p-values (< 0.05 for example) are the ones you are after: smaller p-value means more likely actually differentially expressed genes.

Adjusted p-value or FDR

When we are testing each gene for differential expression ("is gene A differentially expressed between the sample groups? How about gene B? C? D...?") we are actually doing (tens of) thousands of tests, and thus increase the possibility that there are in fact genes in the list of differentially expressed genes just by chance. That is why we need to do "multiple testing correction". There are many ways to do this, one of which is explained in this video at timepoint 21:30:

So, when reporting p-values, you actually want to report the adjusted p-values (sometimes called as the FDR values).
